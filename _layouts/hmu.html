Human Motion Unlearning

arXiv preprint

Edoardo De Matteis, Matteo Migliarini, Alessio Sampieri, Indro Spinelli, Fabio Galasso

Abstract

We introduce the task of human motion unlearning to prevent the synthesis of toxic animations while preserving the general text-to-motion generative performance. Unlearning toxic motions is challenging as those can be generated from explicit text prompts and from implicit toxic combinations of safe motions (e.g., "kicking" is "loading and swinging a leg"). We propose the first motion unlearning benchmark by filtering toxic motions from the large and recent text-to-motion datasets of HumanML3D and Motion-X. We propose baselines, by adapting state-of-the-art image unlearning techniques to process spatio-temporal signals. Finally, we propose a novel motion unlearning model based on Latent Code Replacement, which we dub LCR. LCR is training-free and suitable to the discrete latent spaces of state-of-the-art text-to-motion diffusion models. LCR is simple and consistently outperforms baselines qualitatively and quantitatively.

Implicit Prompt

Our research reveals a critical phenomenon in motion generation technologies: models can inadvertently produce harmful or aggressive content even without explicit instructions. When given neutral movement descriptions, motion synthesis systems may generate sequences that combine safe poses into potentially toxic motion. This implicit toxicity represents a significant ethical challenge, highlighting the need for robust content safeguards and careful algorithmic design in motion generation technologies.

Retain Examples

LCR is capable of unlearning concepts selectively, with minimal degradation on the original model on safe motions. Notably, for safe motion categories, the model demonstrates remarkable robustness, exhibiting minimal performance degradation even with an increased number of code replacements. This observed behavior supports our hypothesis that the LCR approach is highly precise and selective, thereby minimizing potential adverse effects on the model's overall performance.
